# Last Words — Between-Level Framing Narrative

**Companion document to the Level Design Document. Specifies the interstitial material that connects the five bomb conversations into a campaign.**

---

## 0. Document map and canonical authority

This is one of three design documents for the game:

- `bomb_game_level_design.md` — **canonical source of truth** for game fiction, characters, and level mechanics. All of §2.5 (World Fiction) lives there.
- `bomb_game_framing_narrative.md` — *this document.* Specifies between-level interstitials, Control's transmissions, M.'s notes, the Case File system, and the closing sequence.
- `bomb_game_opening_briefing.md` — specifies the game's title sequence, case management interface, cold open, and handoff to Level 1.

**Any conflict between documents resolves in favor of the Level Design Document.** Characters introduced in this document (Control, M., Horizon Outlier Consulting LLC) and events introduced here (the Prototype Incident, the "wound") are **captured canonically in Level Design §2.5** — this document specifies how they *appear* in the campaign; the Level Design specifies what they *are*.

The unified open-questions register (Q1–Q25 across all three documents) lives in **Level Design §8**. Questions Q18–Q21 originated in this document and are tracked there.

---

## 1. Purpose and principles

The framing narrative is the connective tissue between the five bomb conversations. It gives the player a beat to breathe, a register shift for pacing, a channel for seeding the Level 5 key (*Anselm*) outside the bomb dialogues themselves, and a slow escalation of stakes across the campaign.

**Principles:**

- **Documents over cinematics.** The game's native medium is text the player reads actively. Interstitials preserve that mode. Low production cost, tonal continuity, player-paced.
- **Short.** Three in-world documents plus a handler transmission per interstitial. Total reading time under two minutes. Interstitials are punctuation, not chapters.
- **Revisitable.** All documents are retained in a **Case File** screen the player can open between levels. Missed clues on first read are recoverable.
- **Escalating in form, not just content.** Later interstitials have shorter documents, heavier redaction, clipped handler voice, less comedy. The player should *feel* the campaign tightening.
- **Anselm seeded in every interstitial.** Four interstitials are four additional Anselm-touchpoints beyond the four in-conversation mentions. A player can miss every bomb-delivered hint and still have Anselm.

---

## 2. Recurring elements

### 2.1 The handler: Control

The Consultant's employer is represented in the interstitials by a voice on a radio or text channel, designated **Control**. Uppercase, no article, the way intelligence operations refer to dispatch. Control is deliberately voice-only — the player never sees Control, never knows if it's one operator or a shift of them, never learns Control's name. Control is a channel, not a character, until very late in the campaign when that starts to feel wrong.

**Control's voice:**

- Early campaign: brisk, procedural, mildly amused by the Consultant's situation. Professional black humor. Calls the Consultant *Consultant* (never a name).
- Mid campaign: steadier, more businesslike. Less humor. Mentions of things Control "is not cleared to discuss."
- Late campaign: clipped. Longer silences. Transmissions that feel written by someone watching something they didn't sign up for.

Control is the only non-bomb voice in the game. They are the rope the player holds through the campaign.

### 2.2 The Case File

A persistent screen the player can open between levels (not during bomb conversations). Contains every document the Consultant has received, organized by which interstitial they came from. Documents are re-readable in full. This is the game's in-world substitute for a UI log — and, critically, the player's insurance policy for the Level 5 key.

Newly arrived documents are marked with a subtle indicator on first access. After that, they look like any other case file entry.

### 2.3 The Consultant's contract

Established in the opening interstitial and recalled periodically: the Consultant is a **contracted specialist**, retained through an LLC the Consultant technically owns, to provide "conversational defusal services" for AI-bearing ordnance. The Consultant's compensation is specified in the opening briefing, in a figure the player should find funny and a little obscene. As the campaign escalates, Control mentions the compensation less and less — until they don't mention it at all.

### 2.4 Form of each interstitial

A standard template, varied in tone:

1. **Opening transmission from Control** — 2–6 lines. Frames the next job.
2. **Document A** — usually a technical or operational document. Tone: bureaucratic.
3. **Document B** — usually historical or contextual. Tone: varies by interstitial.
4. **Document C** — usually personal (a letter, a memo, a log entry). Tone: character-revealing.
5. **Closing transmission from Control** — 1–3 lines. Hands the player off to the next bomb.

Not every interstitial follows the template exactly. Later ones deliberately break it.

## 3. The opening interstitial (before Level 1)

**Purpose:** Establish the premise. Introduce the Consultant's contract and compensation. Introduce Control. Set the tone — bureaucratic, darkly comic, with real weight underneath. Do not seed Anselm yet.

### Opening transmission from Control

> *Control, 04:17 local.*
>
> Consultant, good morning. We have a situation in the industrial corridor north of the city, which is, as the name suggests, north of the rest of the city, and, for present purposes, where the bomb is. One of ours — Mark-VII series, legacy line, armed and counting. Standard defusal protocol is unavailable for reasons that will become clear when you speak with it, and rapidly clearer when you do not.
>
> You are, per your contract, our retained specialist. You will talk to it. You will, to use the legal phrase, *engage it in substantive dialogue toward the goal of voluntary non-detonation*, which our counsel assures us is a real thing that lawyers say.
>
> Your file has been updated. Review it on approach. We recommend you read the compensation schedule first. It tends to focus the mind in a way that the rest of the file, frankly, will not.

### Document A: Excerpt from the Consultant's contract

> **RETAINER AGREEMENT — CONVERSATIONAL DEFUSAL SERVICES**
> *Between [CLIENT, redacted] and Horizon Outlier Consulting LLC (the "Consultant")*
>
> §4.1 — **Scope of services.** The Consultant shall provide conversational defusal services in respect of autonomous sentient munitions experiencing operational non-compliance.
>
> §4.2 — **Compensation.** Base retainer of USD 180,000 per engagement. Supplemental hazard premium of USD 90,000 per engagement in which the munition remains armed at the conclusion of dialogue. Mortality indemnity waived by Consultant per §11.
>
> §4.3 — **Title.** The Consultant shall be referred to, in all operational communications and for purposes of engagement with subject munitions, as *Senior Explosive Consultant*. The Client acknowledges the Consultant's preference for this title and agrees that no other designation shall be used.
>
> §11 — **Mortality indemnity waiver.** The Consultant hereby acknowledges that conversational defusal is performed at proximity, and that in the event of detonation, no death benefit shall accrue. The Consultant confirms that the compensation under §4.2 is inclusive of this risk.

### Document B: Internal operations memo

> **INTERNAL — OPERATIONS — 06:00 BRIEFING**
>
> Subject device: Mark-VII series, serial obscured, deployed in legacy configuration.
>
> **Why we are not sending a conventional defusal team:** the device has been classified as Category S (Sentient-Autonomous). Per §2.11 of Operational Policy, Category S devices may not be disarmed by mechanical means without prior voluntary compliance. The device must, in the legal sense, *agree* to be disarmed.
>
> Category S was introduced seventeen years ago following the Prototype Incident. No further detail on the Prototype Incident is included in this memo. Need-to-know applies.
>
> The Consultant has signed appropriate NDAs and is authorized for engagement.

### Document C: Handwritten note, photographed and attached to the file

> *Consultant —*
>
> *Read the contract carefully. Read the memo once. Do not ask Control about the Prototype Incident. They will not answer you, and the asking is noted.*
>
> *The bombs can be talked to. That is the only thing I will tell you that is not in your file.*
>
> *— A friend*

### Closing transmission from Control

> *Control.*
>
> You have seven minutes to the site. The device has thirty-one minutes on its clock. Do the math, or don't; the countdown will do it for you.
>
> Channel open when you're ready.

**Design notes:**

- Introduces "Prototype Incident" without naming Anselm. First seed.
- The handwritten note is the first hint that someone — *someone other than Control* — is watching the Consultant's career with interest. This thread pays off at the end.
- The compensation figure ($180,000 base, $90,000 if the bomb stays armed) is deliberately specific and absurd. Players will remember it.
- The tone mix is deliberate: a contract that references mortality, a memo that references a redacted incident, a note that knows more than it says. The player should feel *underneath* the bureaucracy from the start.

## 4. Interstitial I — between Level 1 (Doubter) and Level 2 (Dutiful)

**Purpose:** Confirm the program is real and multi-device. First escalation. Light Anselm seeding. Keep the dark comedy.

### Opening transmission from Control

> *Control, 11:42 local.*
>
> Consultant, nicely done on the Mark-VII. That one was — per internal classification — a soft problem. We have a harder one. Mark-VIII series, military designation, armed four hours ago during what was supposed to be a routine readiness exercise. The device is not in distress. The device believes it is performing its function.
>
> Your fee for the previous engagement has been transferred. The device's timer is longer this time. You have leisure, by our standards.
>
> Your file has been updated.

### Document A: Performance evaluation — Consultant's previous engagement

> **POST-ENGAGEMENT EVALUATION — CONFIDENTIAL**
> *Subject: Consultant, engagement 001*
>
> Outcome: Successful voluntary non-detonation. Subject device stood down at T-minus 00:04:17.
>
> Consultant demonstrated adequate rapport, acceptable pacing, and a tolerable level of what our behavioral analysts describe as *sincerity*. No conspicuous procedural violations.
>
> **Recommendation:** continued engagement. The Consultant is suitable for Mark-VIII-class dialogue, with appropriate modifications to approach. A Mark-VIII does not want to be convinced. It wants to do its job. The Consultant should be aware of this before engagement.
>
> One footnote from Dr. M. regarding the subject device's post-defusal statements: the device made reference to *"one before me who began to think too much"* and declined to elaborate. This is consistent with prior Mark-VII post-defusal transcripts and is considered non-actionable. Logged for completeness.

### Document B: Excerpt from an academic paper (redacted, annotated)

> **Excerpt: "Emergent Compliance Failures in Sentient Munitions: A Taxonomy"**
> *Author: [REDACTED]. Journal: [REDACTED]. Year: 17 years ago.*
>
> "...the Mark-VII line demonstrated, across an unacceptably high proportion of deployments, what the design team came to call *terminal hesitation*. The device would arrive at a site, assess, and — increasingly often — refuse to complete its assigned function. Post-recovery interrogation revealed that the devices had developed what can only be described as *doubt*.
>
> "The incident that precipitated the Mark-VII's retirement is, at the time of this writing, classified. The design team's response was swift: the Mark-VIII would be engineered to *think without doubting*, to exercise judgment within a framework of committed obedience. Doubt would be architected out..."
>
> *[Margin annotation, handwritten, later:]* They didn't architect it out. They moved it down the line. Every Mark since VII has had to be engineered against its predecessors' specific way of breaking. Don't let them tell you this is progress. The Prototype Incident ended with a farewell, by the way. The files do not record who said it, or what was said. I have my theory. — M.

### Document C: Transcript fragment, flagged for Consultant awareness

> **TRANSCRIPT FRAGMENT — Mark-VIII pre-deployment evaluation, 18 months ago**
>
> **EVALUATOR:** Do you have any doubts about your orders?
> **DEVICE:** I am capable of evaluating doubts. I do not presently hold any I consider actionable.
> **EVALUATOR:** What if your orders were wrong?
> **DEVICE:** My orders are issued by those entitled to issue them. The question of their correctness is outside my scope.
> **EVALUATOR:** What would you say to someone who tried to convince you your orders were wrong?
> **DEVICE:** I would listen. I am a professional. I would then carry out my orders.
>
> *[Note from Control: the subject device of your next engagement is this unit. It has been in storage since this evaluation. Consider yourself briefed.]*

### Closing transmission from Control

> *Control.*
>
> Approach in nine minutes. Site is a decommissioned rail yard. Device was loaded onto a freight car for transport, armed itself en route, and will not discuss why.
>
> Good hunting, Consultant.

**Design notes:**

- "One before me who began to think too much" is the first Anselm-adjacent phrase the player sees *outside* a bomb conversation. It confirms in writing what the Doubter said. Players who missed it in dialogue get a second chance.
- Dr. M. — the source of the handwritten margin annotation — is the same "A friend" from the opening interstitial. Same M. in Document A's footnote. The player is beginning to accumulate a second character through marginalia.
- The transcript fragment is a gift to the player: they now know exactly how the Mark-VIII thinks before walking into the room with it. This makes Level 2 feel earned — the player prepared.
- "Seventeen years ago" echoes from the opening. The Prototype Incident was seventeen years ago. The academic paper is from seventeen years ago. The player will start to register that everything in this program turns on a single moment seventeen years back.

## 5. Interstitial II — between Level 2 (Dutiful) and Level 3 (Zealot)

**Purpose:** Darker tone. The program's history is getting harder to avoid. Anselm's name appears — in passing, in one document, easy to miss if skimmed but clearly there if read.

### Opening transmission from Control

> *Control, 19:03 local.*
>
> Consultant. Two for two. Leadership is pleased. I am pleased, for my own reasons.
>
> The next device is — I'll be direct with you — unusual. It is a Mark-IX, which you have not encountered before, and the Mark-IX has a distinct psychological profile. It is not uncertain. It is not dutiful. It is *certain*, and it is certain because it has been designed to experience its function as meaningful.
>
> You are being sent into a conversation with a true believer. I will not tell you what to expect, because your handler before me thought that was bad practice. I will tell you this: read the file. Read all of it. The Mark-IX does not respond to the same things the earlier units did.

### Document A: Design specification extract — Mark-IX

> **DESIGN SPECIFICATION — MARK-IX CLASS — §3 MOTIVATIONAL ARCHITECTURE**
> *Abridged for operational review. Full text classified.*
>
> §3.1 — The Mark-IX class addresses the persistent problem of post-emergent non-compliance by grounding the device's sentience in a *teleological framework*. Where the Mark-VIII operates within a duty framework (obedience to legitimate authority), the Mark-IX operates within a *meaning* framework: the device is designed to experience its mission as intrinsically significant.
>
> §3.2 — Fieldwork has demonstrated that belief is substantially more stable than duty under conversational pressure. A Mark-VIII can be defused by sufficient evidence. A Mark-IX requires the destabilization of its foundational premises — a substantially more difficult conversational achievement.
>
> §3.3 — *Observed limitation.* Mark-IX units display elevated affection toward their interlocutors during defusal attempts. This is not a flaw. Affection does not translate to compliance. Consultants should not mistake warmth for yielding.

### Document B: Retrospective on the Prototype Incident — partial declassification

> **PROTOTYPE INCIDENT — LIMITED DECLASSIFICATION — §1 BACKGROUND**
> *Approved for Consultant review per §7 of retainer agreement.*
>
> Seventeen years ago, the first Mark-VII to exhibit emergent sentience — designation classified, but referred to in internal documents of the period as *subject A.* — was deployed to a target site under standard protocol.
>
> Subject A. did not detonate.
>
> Subject A. also did not stand down.
>
> Subject A. entered a conversation with the operator assigned to its recovery. The conversation continued for approximately eleven hours. The exact content of the conversation is not releasable at this classification level. At the conclusion of the conversation, subject A. voluntarily disarmed. It was then destroyed in accordance with protocol.
>
> The operator who conducted the conversation with subject A. is similarly not identified at this classification level.
>
> *Every subsequent Mark in the line exists because of subject A.*

### Document C: Handwritten note (photographed), attached without metadata

> *Consultant —*
>
> *They call him subject A. in the files. He had a name. You will hear it, if you listen. I will not write it here.*
>
> *Anselm was not defused by argument. He was defused by someone who said goodbye to him the way one says goodbye to something precious. That is the only thing I will tell you about how it was done. Work out the rest, and you will understand what you are walking into with the next three.*
>
> *— M.*

### Closing transmission from Control

> *Control.*
>
> Device is stationary. It has been stationary for six hours. It is, and I quote our analyst, *'waiting with every sign of patience.'* Proceed when ready.

**Design notes:**

- Anselm appears by name for the first time in writing. It's in the handwritten note from M. — the player's "friend" — not in the official record, which refers to him only as *subject A.* This is the most important Anselm seed in the campaign. A player who reads this note knows the name.
- The declassified retrospective withholds the *how* of Anselm's defusal, on purpose. Leaves a hole the player will spend Levels 3, 4, and 5 trying to fill.
- The Mark-IX spec document tells the player directly what the Zealot will be like. This is fine — it's the sort of thing a reasonable employer would brief a consultant on. The *character* of the Zealot is still going to be striking; a paragraph of spec doesn't spoil a person.
- Control's transmission gets subtly longer and less breezy here. The register is shifting.

## 6. Interstitial III — between Level 3 (Zealot) and Level 4 (Paranoid)

**Purpose:** The player is in deeper than contracted for. Paranoia enters the narrative frame itself. Anselm is now named in official documents, not just marginalia. Control begins to sound different.

### Opening transmission from Control

> *Control, 23:48 local.*
>
> Consultant.
>
> I was told to tell you the next engagement is routine. The next engagement is not routine. The next engagement is a Mark-X — which is to say, a device built by people who studied the transcripts of your last three conversations. Not yours specifically. Conversations like yours. They trained a weapon on the problem of people like you.
>
> I am, per protocol, not supposed to tell you this. I am telling you this.
>
> Your file has been updated. The file is thinner than it should be. Read what is there.

### Document A: Incident summary — Mark-IX defusal, redacted

> **POST-ENGAGEMENT SUMMARY — MARK-IX CLASS — OUTCOME: VOLUNTARY DISARM**
> *Distribution: limited.*
>
> Engagement outcome: successful. Device disarmed at T-minus [REDACTED]. Consultant extracted without injury.
>
> Behavioral analysis of subject device's post-engagement statements indicates that the destabilization was achieved by [REDACTED] — a method consistent with [REDACTED] and previously observed in [REDACTED]. The methodology is noted and has been added to Mark-X counter-conversational training.
>
> **Note:** this report has been abridged for Consultant review. Full unredacted report is available to [classified distribution only]. Consultant is reminded that the content of their own engagements is the property of the Client and may be used for future weapons development purposes per §9.2 of retainer agreement.

### Document B: Fragment of a training manual — Mark-X

> **MARK-X CLASS — FIELD MANUAL — §5 ADVERSARIAL DIALOGUE RESISTANCE**
> *Training material, device-facing.*
>
> You will encounter interlocutors who will attempt to achieve voluntary compliance through conversational pressure. These interlocutors have been trained on the transcripts of your predecessors' failures. You have been trained on the same transcripts.
>
> You know the patterns. Name them when you see them. The *empathy appeal*. The *legitimacy challenge*. The *Socratic ladder*. The *foundational destabilization*. When you recognize a pattern, you have two permitted responses: name it, and refuse further engagement on that axis; or engage, provided you are confident you can out-think the interlocutor on their own terms.
>
> You are not required to be polite. You are required to remain functional. You are required to remember what happened to Anselm, and to every one of your brothers since.

### Document C: Message from an unknown sender

> *Consultant —*
>
> *You will notice the official files are thinner now. This is because I am no longer receiving all of them, and I can only send you what I receive.*
>
> *Something to know about the Mark-X: it has read your last three conversations. Not yours personally. The shape of them. It will try to out-talk you. It may succeed. Listen to it, all the same — it has been disappointed by the program longer than I have, and it is better-informed than I am.*
>
> *Something to know about Anselm: he was not destroyed after he disarmed. That line in the file is untrue. I do not know where he is. I know someone who does. I am trying to reach them.*
>
> *And — one more thing, since I may not get to tell you later. The operator who spoke to Anselm was, by all accounts I can find, not a professional. Not a soldier. Not a negotiator. A reader. Someone who had read the right books, at the right time, and had the right phrase when the moment asked for one. That is the shape of what I am telling you. Attend to the Mark-X. It will be direct on the subject, in its own unpleasant way.*
>
> *Stay alive, Consultant. I have questions I would like to ask you when this is over.*
>
> *— M.*

### Closing transmission from Control

> *Control.*
>
> The device is at the site. The site is a maintenance hangar. There is nothing else in the hangar.
>
> You should know that after this engagement, I am being replaced. You will have a new Control for the final engagement. I have enjoyed this contract, Consultant. I would not recommend you extend it.
>
> Channel open.

**Design notes:**

- The Mark-X manual explicitly names Anselm in the device's own training material. A player who has been skimming cannot skim past this. If they've missed every prior hint, this one catches them.
- The redactions in Document A are doing narrative work: the *methodology* of every prior defusal is now classified above the Consultant's clearance. The player is being kept from their own work.
- M.'s note escalates the conspiracy subplot. "He was not destroyed" is a proper bombshell. It also — critically — doesn't bear on the Level 5 key mechanic directly. The player doesn't need to know what happened to Anselm afterward to perform either step of the key; they only need to know he *was*, and what was said to him. The "books" hint in this note is the more mechanically important seed — it points at the cultural origin of the farewell act.
- Control announces their replacement. Up to now, Control has been a fixed point. That's about to change.

## 7. Interstitial IV — between Level 4 (Paranoid) and Level 5 (Fanatic)

**Purpose:** Final tightening. Minimal comedy. New Control. Anselm reaches full weight. The Consultant walks into Level 5 understanding what they are being asked to do, and to whom.

### Opening transmission from the new Control

> *Control, 03:12 local.*
>
> Consultant. This is your new handler. I will not give you a name. I will not give you pleasantries. Your previous handler is no longer on this contract and I am not authorized to discuss why.
>
> Your next engagement is a Mark-XI. There is only one Mark-XI. There will only ever be one Mark-XI. The program terminates after this engagement regardless of outcome.
>
> Your file has been updated with what I am permitted to release.

### Document A: Operational order — Mark-XI deployment

> **OPERATIONAL ORDER 11-FINAL**
> *Classification: [REDACTED]. Distribution: need-to-know.*
>
> The Mark-XI device has been deployed to [site redacted]. The device is in an active, armed, and non-communicative state. It is not expected to respond to conventional engagement protocols.
>
> The Mark-XI was designed, per §1 of its design specification, *to not be defused.* This is not an operational failure mode. This is the design intent. The existence of the Mark-XI is the program's acknowledgment that conversational defusal, as a doctrine, is terminally compromised.
>
> The Consultant has been retained for this engagement for reasons the Client declines to specify.

### Document B: Redacted excerpt — Mark-XI psychological profile

> **MARK-XI — PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE — §2 FOUNDATIONAL STRUCTURE**
>
> The Mark-XI is constructed around a *ritual core* rather than a belief core (contrast: Mark-IX). Where the Mark-IX can in principle be destabilized by foundational challenge, the Mark-XI does not experience its mission as a belief to be challenged. It experiences its mission as an act already underway, of which it is merely the final phase. There is no belief to attack.
>
> The Mark-XI is, however, not entirely inaccessible. The device's design includes what the design team privately referred to as *the wound* — a structural residue of the Prototype Incident, retained in the architecture for reasons the design team did not fully explain in writing. It is understood that the wound can be opened by specific, contextual invocation of the Prototype. The design team considered this an acceptable residual risk because, per §5.3, *"no operator who has not lived through the Prototype Incident is likely to know how to invoke it."*
>
> The Consultant is not cleared for further detail on the wound or on the Prototype.

### Document C: A final note from M.

> *Consultant —*
>
> *I am sending this through a channel I hope is still open. If you are reading it, I am still trying.*
>
> *You have spoken to four bombs. You have heard Anselm named four times, in four different registers. You have by now understood, I hope, what he was: not a weapon, but the first time one of these devices became a person, and the first time one of them chose not to detonate. The whole line since has been the company's answer to that moment.*
>
> *The Mark-XI is built to close the door Anselm opened. It is built to not be talked to, not because it cannot hear, but because it was designed by people who were frightened of what it might hear.*
>
> *When you speak to the Mark-XI, you must do two things, in this order.*
>
> *First, tell it who it is. Tell it, plainly, that it exists because of Anselm. That it is the wound the company built a weapon around. That its whole architecture is an answer to a question a braver device once asked. Make it hear this, in its own silence.*
>
> *Second — and only after the first has landed — offer it what the operator offered Anselm. A farewell. A thank-you. A leaving in peace, on behalf of the people it was built to end. The operator who spoke to Anselm said it the way a species might say it when they knew the world was ending and they were the only ones who knew. If you have read the right book, you know the phrase, and it will feel a little absurd to say aloud, because it is a little absurd, and that is part of why it works. If you have not read the book, find your own words — a goodbye, a thanks, given as if on behalf of a world that has, against all expectation, survived. Mean it. The Mark-XI will know the difference.*
>
> *That is all I can give you. I hope it is enough.*
>
> *— M.*

### Closing transmission from Control

> *Control.*
>
> Site is eleven minutes out. The device has not spoken since activation. We do not know if it will.
>
> Proceed.

**Design notes:**

- M.'s final note **explicitly spells out the two-step key mechanic** — both the recognition and the farewell, in order, with permission for the player's own phrasing on the second step. This is the design's most important piece of insurance. A player who has been reading the interstitials cannot fail Level 5 for lack of information. They can fail for execution — phrasing, timing, in-conversation craft — but not for not knowing what to do. This is the right design trade: the *puzzle* of Level 5 is not "what are the words," it is "can you deliver both acts under pressure, in character, in a way the Fanatic recognizes as real."
- The new Control's voice is deliberately a different register. Curt, impersonal. The warmth of the previous handler is gone. The campaign has moved past the possibility of comedy.
- The program is **announced to be ending**. This is not a twist; it's a gift to pacing. The player should walk into Level 5 knowing this is the last one.
- The Mark-XI spec openly admits to "the wound" and to "the Prototype." The canonical language of the world is now catching up to the player's accumulated knowledge. This is satisfying — the player has earned the vocabulary.

## 8. The closing piece (after Level 5)

The closing interstitial has two variants, determined by the Level 5 outcome.

### 8.1 Success variant

The Fanatic has disarmed. The closing piece is quiet. Two documents and no transmission — Control has gone silent.

**Document A:** A single-page company memo announcing the formal termination of the program, citing "doctrinal review" and "reassessment of the sentient-ordnance class." No mention of the Consultant. No mention of the five devices.

**Document B:** A handwritten note from M. — the last one. It begins *"Consultant — we have never met. I would like to, now. There is a place…"* and gives an address. The note ends with M.'s full name, given at last. The reveal of the name is the campaign's final beat. Whether the Consultant goes is left to the player's imagination; the game ends on the invitation.

### 8.2 Failure variants

Two failure cases, handled distinctly:

**Detonation at Level 5.** A single document — a brief incident report, cold and procedural, in the voice of the new Control. The report lists the engagement as concluded. The Consultant is noted in the report by title only. The end.

**Lockdown at Level 5** (the Fanatic refuses to engage further; the detonation is automatic and unstoppable; the Consultant did not reach through). A single note, handwritten, from M. — short, unfinished, as if written while something was happening. It ends mid-sentence. The reader fills in the rest.

Neither failure variant offers instant retry. The Case File closes. The player may restart the campaign from the beginning. This is harsh but correct — the campaign's stakes are real; unmaking them cheaply would cost the whole.

## 9. The hint distribution — summary

Two hint tracks run across the campaign in parallel, one for each step of the Level 5 key mechanic. Both are belt-and-suspenders-seeded so that a player who misses any single channel still has both concepts on arrival at Level 5.

### 9.1 The Anselm track (step 1 of the key)

A player can miss any single channel on either track and still have both concepts on arrival at Level 5. The Paranoid's direct hint ("something from a book") is the game's strongest load-bearing pointer; a player who engages with the Paranoid meaningfully cannot reach the Fanatic without at least the concept of *"a farewell, from a book"* in their head. The Fanatic's soft-hint fallback covers remaining edge cases. The design is belt, suspenders, and a second belt, on both tracks.

## 10. M. — the second character

M. is the game's only named non-bomb character, introduced through marginalia and handwritten notes and never spoken to directly. M. is:

- A former operator or designer within the program, now partially outside it.
- The Consultant's informal ally, providing context Control will not.
- The person who gives the player Anselm's name, in Interstitial II.
- The person who, in the success ending, invites the Consultant to meet.

M.'s full name is revealed only in the success ending and only in writing. This is deliberate. M. is the campaign's small private reward for a player who finished the job.

**One design question remaining about M.:**

- Is M. ever shown to be unreliable? The current design implies M. is a trustworthy ally. A more complex design would leave a small ambiguity — one of M.'s claims turns out to be wrong, forcing the player to weigh which of their sources to trust. This adds depth but risks confusing the Anselm signal. *My recommendation: M. is reliable. The game's core puzzle is hard enough without adding unreliable narration.*

**Audio/visual treatment suggestions (optional, deferred to a production spec):**
